LERU map pilot

Mapping the visible and hidden machinery of research integrity in Europe

This report explains the working logic of the LERU map pilot and highlights what the current evidence is already showing: European research integrity systems are not just national bodies. They are routes, committees, funders, institutional rules, adjacent regulators, public archives, annual reports, and gaps.

Executive argument

The project is a research instrument, not only a map.

The static app is the public face of a larger evidence system. Each country profile condenses legal texts, body pages, university procedures, funder requirements, quality-assurance files, committee outputs, public case archives, and local source notes into a comparable profile. The aim is to preserve national specificity while making comparison possible.

The key message for a presentation

The strongest question is not "does a country have a research integrity body?" It is "where does a concern enter the system, who handles it, who decides, who publishes, and what public evidence remains?"

How it works

A five-step pipeline turns scattered documentation into comparable country dossiers.

The workflow is deliberately conservative. It starts with local overview documents and official sources, separates adjacent regimes from misconduct handling, and records missingness instead of forcing each country into a single template.

Collect the source trail

Gather laws, ministry pages, body websites, university procedures, funder conditions, annual reports, registers, and local source notes.

Draft the country profile

Normalize each country into system type, summary, key actors, document classes, data signals, next focus, and source links.

Map the handling network

Identify where concerns start, what committees or offices receive them, who makes final decisions, and which funders or bodies add consequences.

Separate boundary regimes

Keep research ethics approval, clinical trials, animal research, data protection, IP, QA, accreditation, and open science from general misconduct findings.

Code public visibility

Record whether evidence appears as decisions, opinions, case summaries, annual statistics, institutional fragments, procedure pages, or no located publication route.

Data model

The app compares systems by routes and evidence, not by slogans.

A country can look centralized in law but distributed in practice. Another can have no national misconduct archive but strong institutional publication. The dossier structure is built to capture that difference.

Why the distinction matters

Without route-level coding, a clinical-trial register, accreditation decision, or plagiarism procedure can be mistaken for general research-misconduct transparency.

What makes a deep dossier

A deep dossier has a source backbone, system map, network layers, committee directory, boundary map, timeline, and explicit transparency assessment. The current corpus has moved from overview extraction into committee-and-case-file discovery, which is where the strongest comparative findings are emerging.

The approach also makes uncertainty usable. "No public case archive identified" is not a failure of the profile; it is a finding about public accountability.

Current interesting findings

What the evidence is already telling us.

The strongest findings are methodological and substantive at the same time. They change how a European comparison should be framed.

1. There is no single European model.

Some countries have national boards with public decisions. Others rely on academies, funders, institutional first-line procedures, or statutory ethics councils. A clean comparison needs to show whether the core is legal, soft-law, institutional, funder-led, attestation-led, or distributed across several layers.

2. Transparency is often not where the formal route is.

In several systems, the body that receives or investigates a concern is not the body that publishes public evidence. Publication may sit with a second-line body, a sector association, a funder, a university board, an annual report, or a quality-assurance corridor.

3. Institutional micro-publication is a major evidence class.

Local committee opinions, university annual-report rows, ombuds statements, faculty-level decisions, and isolated case notices often provide stronger evidence than national pages. These fragments matter because they show how the route actually operates.

4. Adjacent regimes can look more transparent than misconduct handling.

Clinical-trial registers, bioethics approval lists, animal-research authorizations, data-protection decisions, IP disputes, and accreditation files can be very visible. The report keeps them separate so the map does not overstate research-misconduct publication.

5. No archive is still a finding.

For countries where no public case archive was located, the app records the strongest available route: statutory body, institutional procedure, QA output, annual statistics, or boundary evidence. This lets the corpus distinguish missing publication from missing governance.

6. The project is moving from coverage to calibration.

The first job was to cover the map. The current job is to refine the method: route scope, archive scope, publication owner, accessibility, retention, public data elements, outcome maturity, and the difference between aggregate monitoring and case-file publication.

  • Code restricted, unstable, inaccessible, or no-publication evidence explicitly.

Presentation path

A useful way to present the project in 10 minutes.

Start with the problem, show the instrument, then use a few contrasting countries to make the findings memorable.

Suggested talk track

  1. Open with the premise: research integrity systems are visible unevenly, and visibility is itself a governance feature.
  2. Show the map and country list in index.html.
  3. Open one structured case-publication system, such as the Netherlands, Denmark, Finland, Sweden, or Ukraine.
  4. Contrast it with a distributed or low-publication system, such as Kosovo, Iceland, Malta, or Greece.
  5. Explain why boundary lanes must stay separate: bioethics, QA, animal research, data protection, IP, and open science do not equal misconduct handling.
  6. Close with the next phase: finalize missing overviews, widen final sweeps, and standardize route-level case-file coding.

What to emphasize

The app is already useful because it makes comparison possible without erasing complexity. It does not claim that all countries should have the same model. It asks where accountability is visible and what kind of public source supports that judgement.

What not to overclaim

Transparency scores are not system-quality scores. They measure public evidence of case handling. A low-publication system may still have real internal procedures, while a visible boundary register may not be a misconduct route.

Good live searches

In the app, search for terms such as LOWI, annual report, ethics committee, funder, archive, bioethics, data protection, or quality assurance. These searches reveal how the same issue travels through different national architectures.

Best closing line

The map is not just asking who has a research integrity body. It is asking what happens when a concern becomes a file, and whether the public can see any trace of it.

Limitations

The current findings should be presented with explicit guardrails.

Language and access limits

Some official sources are hard to reach, unstable, blocked, dynamic, or available only in national-language interfaces. The corpus records those limits rather than smoothing them away.

Ongoing source collection

Several countries still need final sweeps for institutional annual reports, public committee outputs, funder consequences, and boundary-lane separation.

No quality ranking

The transparency layer measures publication visibility, not fairness, speed, independence, investigative quality, or the culture of research integrity in a country.

Evidence can move

Web archives, annual reports, committee pages, and register outputs can change. The source registry and local documentation folders are part of the audit trail for later reconciliation.
