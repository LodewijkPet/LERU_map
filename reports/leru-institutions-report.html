Executive summary

Public-source coverage and transparency signals

This public-source report represents the official LERU member universities and maps the visible research-integrity routes, offices, committees, procedure documents and public reporting identified in this pass. It is designed to help member review, not to function as an official LERU statement or an institutional audit.

Key findings

What the current public-source draft shows

  • LERU institutions are generally procedure-visible: each member profile now has identifiable public institutional route or procedure evidence.
  • Public transparency is more uneven than procedure visibility: visible routes do not always lead to public annual reports, aggregate statistics or anonymized outputs.
  • National, regional and sector-level reporting can make institution-level comparability harder, especially where local handling is not reported separately.
  • Annual statements, aggregate reports and anonymized summaries are more common than full case-file archives.
  • Boundary regimes are often visible, but ethics approval, clinical governance, animal research, data protection, quality assurance, student discipline, IP/open science and research security should not be confused with research-misconduct handling unless the source explicitly connects them.
  • Member review is needed to confirm internal routes, current office names, non-public reporting practices and any public sources missed in this pass.

Reading the system rather than isolated pages

Systems and routes

Country-level systems shape what is visible at institutional level. Some LERU members sit in national or sector frameworks with second-line bodies, public funder output or annual-statement expectations; others rely mainly on institution-owned procedures, local ombuds routes, scientific-integrity referents, committees or executive-board decisions.

The comparative matrix therefore separates national bodies and funder lanes from local committees, offices, ombudspersons and referents. The same country can contain both output-rich and output-light institutional examples.

Outputs and boundaries

Output models range from institution-owned annual reports, anonymized statements and report tables to national summaries, restricted internal reporting, historical case-complex pages and procedure-only visibility. These are different transparency signals and should not be collapsed into one score.

Boundary regimes can create strong public evidence, but they remain adjacent unless explicitly linked to research-misconduct handling. This includes research ethics, clinical trials, animal research, data protection, quality assurance, student discipline, IP/open science and research security.

Limitations

What this draft does not claim

  • This is a public-source draft based on official pages, public documents and existing project data; it is not an official LERU statement.
  • Search, language and source-currency limitations remain. Some pages may have changed, moved or remained inaccessible in this pass.
  • The report does not claim exhaustiveness and should not be interpreted as an institutional audit.
  • Absence of public output should not be interpreted as absence of cases, concerns, investigations or internal reporting.
  • Institutional and national levels may overlap; national or sector output is not automatically institution-owned output.
  • Research ethics, data protection, clinical ethics, animal research, student discipline, quality assurance, IP/open science and research security are boundary regimes unless explicitly connected to research-misconduct handling.

How to read profiles

What each institution profile is trying to show

Institutional routeWhere a concern about research integrity appears to enter the university process, for example through an ombudsperson, research-integrity office, named adviser, committee or executive-board route.
Rule basisThe code, regulation, procedure or national framework that appears to give the route its authority. Sometimes the same document is both the regulation and the rule basis.
Public reportingWhether public sources show annual statements, aggregate statistics, anonymized summaries, public decisions or only the procedure itself.
Committee historyWhat public sources say about how long the route has been visible and what activity is publicly described, such as annual reports, case counts or types of allegations.
Adjacent governanceEthics review, clinical trials, animal research, data protection, quality assurance, IP, open science and research security can be important, but they are not treated as misconduct handling unless the source explicitly connects them.
Source checklistEach profile lists the same core source types first: committee or route page, country dossier, regulation, rule basis, annual-report location and case repository or public-decision location.

Next step phase

Questions for LERU / INTE member review

The next phase is a member review phase. These questions are meant to check whether the public-source reading matches the way institutions understand their own route, reporting practice and public/private boundaries.

  • Is this the correct public institutional route for research-integrity concerns?
  • Is the named office, committee, ombudsperson, adviser network or referent current?
  • Are annual reports, aggregate statistics, anonymized summaries or case-learning outputs public, internal or absent?
  • Are national, regional or sector reports interpreted correctly in relation to the institution?
  • Which operational details should remain unpublished in a public member-review product?
  • Which public sources should be added, corrected, replaced or retired?
